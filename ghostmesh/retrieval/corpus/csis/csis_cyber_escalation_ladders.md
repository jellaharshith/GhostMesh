CSIS research on cyber escalation identifies that most states operate with implicit "escalation ladders" — graduated response options that stop short of conflict thresholds while imposing increasing costs. Critical finding: adversaries monitor Blue Team response tempo and capability to calibrate their next move.

Three adversary escalation patterns identified:
1. Probe-and-pause: adversary tests Blue detection capability with a low-consequence action, then waits for response before committing to next stage. Blue's failure to detect probe = escalation signal.
2. Opportunistic acceleration: when Blue is distracted (incident response, maintenance window, organizational change), adversary accelerates lateral movement. Blue Team action that creates a predictable "eyes-off" window is an escalation invitation.
3. Deterrence signaling: adversary makes a detectable but reversible move (e.g., touching HMI-01 without executing payload) to signal capability and deter Blue from certain actions. This is a negotiating move, not an attack.

Counter-escalation principle: Blue response must close the "signal window" — acknowledge detected capability without revealing full detection scope. Partial disclosure of findings may be preferable to full disclosure in some scenarios.

Key metric: adversary re-entry rate after eviction predicts geopolitical commitment level. Single re-entry = opportunistic. Multiple re-entries after clean eviction = strategic mission, hardened objective.
