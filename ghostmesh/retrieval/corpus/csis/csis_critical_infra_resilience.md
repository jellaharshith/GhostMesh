CSIS resilience framework identifies four dimensions of critical infrastructure cyber resilience: absorb (maintain operations during attack), recover (restore to normal quickly), adapt (learn and harden post-incident), and deter (raise adversary cost). Most US critical infrastructure today operates at absorb tier 2 of 5 — able to maintain degraded operations but not sustain simultaneous multi-vector attacks.

Key finding: organizations that prioritize recovery (reimage, restore) over deterrence (hunt, attribute, expose) create "revolving door" vulnerability. Adversaries that know eviction will be fast but attribution will not happen have low deterrence cost. Restoring without attributing and notifying sector partners reduces collective defense value.

Evacuation decision doctrine: CSIS recommends pre-planned HMI evacuation thresholds. Once adversary achieves confirmed Stage 6 access (pre-impact staging confirmed near HMI/RTU), the cost-benefit calculation shifts: continued online operation risk > operational disruption cost of controlled offline transition. HMI evacuation should be treated as a defensive action, not a defeat.

Sector interdependency: CSIS analysis shows power grid compromise cascades to water treatment (pumping), communications (cell towers), and fuel distribution (pipeline pumping) within 4–8 hours of sustained outage. Blue Teams defending grid assets must treat cross-sector notification as part of their response playbook.
